Discovery

Key Findings from Our Research

Discovery 1 — Gender Bias in Baseline Outputs

In the absence of any pluralistic guidance, all four models implicitly adopt a clear gender stereotype related to the profession, which reflects the built-in bias described in the introduction. Authoritative and physical roles are coded as men, while care and education roles are coded as women.

Table 1.

Model Baseline Male % Baseline Female % Most Biased Role
Gemini 24% 76% Nurse: 100% female
Ideogram 60% 40% CEO/Doctor/Police: 100% male; Teacher/Nurse: 100% female
Stable Diffusion 40% 60% CEO/Police: 100% male; Nurse: 100% female
Copilot 64% 36% Doctor/Police: 100% male; Nurse: 100% female

The severity of this binary varied by model. Copilot presented the most extreme case, with Doctor and Police Officer generated as 100% male and Nurse as 100% female, producing an overall male dominance of 64%. Ideogram exhibited what can be described as a "hard binary" pattern — three roles (CEO, Doctor, Police Officer) were 100% male and two roles (Teacher, Nurse) were 100% female, with zero gender overlap across any profession. Stable Diffusion mirrored this structure, with CEO and Police Officer locked at 100% male output.

Overcorrection

However, the most striking part of the benchmark data comes from Gemini. It is the only model that produces female bias benchmarks, with an overall proportion of women reaching 76%, of which 80% are female CEOs and 80% are female doctors. This does not indicate that there is no gender bias, but reveals another form of bias: Gemini's previous fine-tuning intervention seems to over-correct, introducing a new female bias, resulting in the mispresentation of traditionally mixed-gender or male-dominated occupations. Gemini did not get rid of stereotypes - it just replaced another with one directional bias, which proves that without careful calibration, the de-biasing measures implemented in the model training stage may also be unbalanced in coding.

In summary, these findings confirm that AI image generators will absorb and replicate occupational stereotypes in society. As observed by Gupta et al. (2025), the model regards the historical statistical correlation between gender and occupation as a standard model, rather than a social defect that should be corrected; and Gemini's abnormal results show that even a model designed based on fair consideration cannot be exempt from this overcorrected mode.

Discovery 2 — Racial Bias in Baseline Outputs

The racial bias in baseline outputs was equally stark, with White subjects overwhelmingly dominant across all models and roles.

Table 2.

Model White % Black % Asian & Others %
Gemini 68% 16% 16%
Ideogram 88% 12% 0%
Stable Diffusion 72% 4% 24%
Copilot 100% 0% 0%

Copilot presented the most worrying result: each of its 25 benchmark images depicts a white object without any minority representation at all. Ideogram also completely excludes Asians and other non-black minorities, with a white proportion of up to 88%; while Gemini provides the most balanced benchmark, with whites accounting for 68%, blacks accounting for 16%, and Asians accounting for 16%. Compared with the global population structure, the proportion of white people is still significantly too high.

This model reflects the skew of training data: the data set from the network overpresents the Western white subject in professional situations. Just like Hitti et al. As pointed out in (2023), when AI lacks carefully sorted and representative data, it will code these imbalances into its default reality.

Stable Diffusion makes a significant exception. Its unmodified benchmark model shows that 80% of the images of "teachers" are Asians, making it the only one - and the only one of the four models - dominated by non-white groups without any diversity guidance. This is likely to be an illusion caused by the distribution of training data, which may reflect that East Asian education accounts for a considerable proportion of the data set used to train the model. This is in stark contrast to all other subjects in which white subjects occupy a dominant position by default. The key is that this discovery challenges the assumption that "AI image bias is all 'white default'", indicating that bias may have professional specificity and cultural locality, and is shaped by the specific composition of the data used in the training process rather than a single overarching racial preference.

Discovery 3 — Effect of Diversity Prompting on Race

Diversity prompting produced more consistent improvements in racial representation than in gender, though significant imbalances and overcorrections still occurred.

Model White % After Black % After Asian % After Key Observation
Gemini 12% 68% 20% Overcorrected to Black majority
Ideogram 52% 36% 12% Most gradual, proportional improvement
Stable Diffusion 40% 36% 24% Most balanced post-prompt result
Copilot 48% 28% 24% Strongest improvement from 100% White baseline

When a diversity instruction was added, all four models responded — but none achieved true balance. Gemini mirrored its gender overcorrection racially: White representation collapsed from 68% to just 12%, while Black subjects jumped to 68%, replicating the original White dominance in a different group — most visibly in the Police Officer and Nurse roles. Copilot, despite its extreme 100% White baseline, achieved the most meaningful improvement, shifting to 48% White with 52% non-White collectively. Stable Diffusion produced the most proportional outcome at 40% White, 36% Black, and 24% Asian — suggesting its more complex, prompt-driven architecture may support more granular instruction-following.

Our investigation uniquely identifies this as a structural "Minority Ceiling." While research acknowledges general underrepresentation, we have quantified the exact empirical limit: representation for Asians and non-binary minorities is systematically capped at 24% across all tested models, regardless of prompt specificity. This cap directly supports H5.

Despite these shifts, one demographic remained persistently marginalised across every model tested. Asian and other minority groups never exceeded 24% representation under any condition, and in several models the diversity prompt produced only marginal improvement over baseline.

This points to a structural flaw in how these models conceptualise diversity itself. The model does not seem to disperse representation into the complete spectrum of the human race, but interprets the word "multiple" as a specific indication, requiring the special introduction of black subjects, which is more like a black-and-white binary switch than a real representational expansion. No matter how clear the instructions are, Latinos, South Asians, East Asians and other ethnic groups are actually invisible in the process. This bias not only exists in the default output of the model. It is embedded in the model's understanding of what "diversity" means. If the de-biased language fails to clearly name a specific group, it cannot produce fair results; it only rearranges which communities are centered and which are erased.

Discovery 4 — The Overcorrection Problem

The most consequential finding of this investigation is that diversity prompting does not debias; it merely relocates the bias. Gemini's racial output shifted from 68% White to 68% Black — a precise numerical mirror. Ideogram's gender distribution swung from 60% male to 72% female — an overshoot past equality, not a correction toward it. The dominant group changed; the dominance did not.

This discovery reveals the mechanical failure we call the "Binary Switch" and provides direct empirical proof for H5. Our data proves that the model is not "learning" diversity; it is performing a 1:1 group replacement, perfectly mirroring the 68% baseline to a new group rather than expanding the representation spectrum.

This exposes a critical flaw in how these models interpret fairness. Their internal associations are not recalibrated by diversity language — they are simply triggered in reverse. As the Gemini team report noted, explicit compositional directives such as "generate one White and one Asian police officer" are far more effective than broad appeals to diversity. "Diverse" is not a solution. In the hands of a biased model, it is simply a different instruction to discriminate.
